What is digital accessibility?

Process of removing and preventing barriers to access so everyone can enjoy your work equally.

Physical

  • Muscle control limitations
  • Joint disorders
  • Missing limbs

Auditory

Partial or complete loss of hearing in one or both ears

Visual

  • Partial vision loss ("low vision")
  • Complete loss ("blindness")
  • Reduced or increased sensitivity to certain colors ("color blindness")

Cognitive

Cognitive, learning, and neurological disorders including neurodiversity, neurological, behavioral, and mental health disorders.

Temporary

Broken limbs, vertigo or fatigue after medical procedures, recovery from accidents, undergoing physical theraphy.

Circumstantial

High-stress environments like disaster/accident scenarios, distraction, broken trackpad/mouse forcing keyboard use, ESL users.

Why does it matter?

Its the right thing to do!

Legal landscape

  • Rehabilitation Act of 1973

  • Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

    Public and commercial contexts

  • Section 508 and 504

    Government contexts and groups receiving federal aid (like public schools, health care, etc)

For businesses

  • Reduce risk of legal action (demand letters, lawsuits, etc)
  • Considering accessibility throughout the SDLC is much easier and cheaper than trying to hack and layer it in during a crisis.
  • Reach a larger market share, potentially cornering a market segment that has been underserved.
  • Improve company image by engaging in objectively positive, socially responsible activity.

Spend money on talent, not lawyers!

For developers

  • Because it relies heavily on using native tech as intended, it naturally encourages cleaner code that is easier to maintain.
  • Sought after skill in modern market, and is increasing in value right now
  • Personal and professional growth. Learning new things is important.

How do I build accessible stuff?

WCAG to the rescue!

This slide has GIF background image of a chihuahua wearing a Superman costume walking on a treadmill

12 guidelines based on 4 principles

  • Perceivable - everyone can access the content
  • Operable - everyone can interact with it
  • Understandable - UI and content is clear
  • Robust - works with many user agents + AT

Use HTML the way it was designed

Use semantic, native, and valid markup as much as possible

Use ARIA, but only when absolutely necessary

Testing for accessibility

  • Use only your keyboard to get a decent idea of operability, focus indicators, and focus management.
  • Use mobile devices. They are very popular because of TalkBack and VoiceOver's Rotor.
  • Use extreme zoom and check for content overflows.

Automated tools only catch about 40% of issues
According to research from GOV.UK

Good for:

  • Identifying simple, clear syntax issues like invalid or bad markup use
  • Flagging potential issues for investigation by testers
  • Regression testing to alert devs about potential issues to check

Bad for:

  • Checking for overuse of ARIA
  • Misuse of semantic elements given context
  • Accurate, useful image descriptions
  • UX for people with disabilities - do they enjoy your site?

In court, nothing beats live human testing!

What can
you do?

  • Advocate for accessibility as a core feature during requirements gathering and planning
  • Bake accessibility solutions and guidance in your pattern library or design framework
  • Create and use personas of people with disabilities in your design process
  • Set up monthly Keyboard Only or Screen Reader Only days
  • Collaborate with people with disabilities - user testing, lunch and learns, demo days
  • Empower accessibility champions in multiple areas - start with informal SMEs, grow from there
  • Reach out to companies like Accessible360!

Hire more people with disabilities!
